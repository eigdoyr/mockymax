# v0.2 Viability Test — Findings

Issue #34. Generated 25 prompts across 4 device categories to evaluate whether AI-generated scenes can hit MockyMax's quality bar. Total cost ~$1.50.

## Results

| Category    | Keep + Maybe | Drop   | Keep rate |
| ----------- | ------------ | ------ | --------- |
| Monitors    | 1            | 4      | 20%       |
| Phones      | 9            | 1      | 70%       |
| Tablets     | 2            | 3      | 40%       |
| Laptops     | 3            | 2      | 60%       |
| **Overall** | **15**       | **10** | **~55%**  |

Threshold for category viability: 30% keep rate.

## What worked

**Editorial language.** Round 1 used product-photography keywords ("8k, photorealistic, professional product photography"). Round 2 used editorial-fashion language ("Kinfolk Magazine still life, intimate body fragment, chiaroscuro lighting"). Round 2 keep rates were dramatically higher.

**Body-fragment phone compositions.** Prompts that included a hand, a lap, a pocket, a wrist — without showing the full body — produced the strongest results.

**Keyboard-hiding laptop techniques.** Alcove with shadow on the keyboard, extreme crop above the keyboard line, three-quarter angled foreshortening — all three approaches successfully hid AI keyboard artifacts.

## What didn't work

**Brand-logo hallucination on monitors.** Even with explicit "no logos, no brand markings" in the prompt, Flux Pro 1.1 Ultra renders fake brand wordmarks on monitor bezels. Negative prompts do not reliably suppress text/logo generation in this model.

**Hand counting on tablets.** One tablet-with-hands prompt produced three hands. Diffusion models still struggle with hand count and finger geometry.

**Specific iPhone styling.** Phones rendered with notches, dynamic islands, and other Apple-specific design cues even when prompted as unbranded. Pattern-matching to training data dominates.

**Awkward angles for compositing.** Some images that were beautiful aesthetically had screens angled away from camera or too small in the frame to make useful composite targets. Editorial composition and compositor-friendly geometry sometimes conflict.

## Decisions for v0.2 production

Scene count targets for issue #37 (generate and ship v0.2 library):

- **Phones:** 10-12 final scenes (highest keep rate, biggest demand)
- **Laptops:** 4-5 final scenes (surprised on the upside)
- **Tablets:** 3-4 final scenes (selective)
- **Monitors:** 3-4 final scenes (extra prompt iteration needed)

**Total target: 20-25 scenes.** Lower than the original 20-30 target but quality > quantity.

## Prompt principles validated

- Editorial-fashion language over product-photography language
- Specific real-photographer/magazine style references (Kinfolk, Wallpaper, Aesop campaigns) — Flux honors these
- Composition spec (angle, position, frame placement) per prompt
- Lighting variety beyond "soft natural light"
- Body-as-subject for phone/tablet
- Heavy shadow / extreme crop / three-quarter foreshortening for laptops

## Prompt anti-patterns to avoid

- "8k, photorealistic, professional product photography" — biases toward stock-photo aesthetic
- Generic "minimal aesthetic" without supporting composition direction
- "Empty scene with no other objects" — leads to flat compositions
- Trying to suppress logos/text with negative phrasing alone

## Open questions for v0.3+

- Can a curated 3D phone asset be composited into AI scenes for better device control?
